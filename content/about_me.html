A brief recap of my professional journey

A summary of myself, my interests and achivements.

Santiago Noacco

Where did it all began

Born and raised in Patagonia, Argentina, I grew up deeply connected to nature, which I believe instilled in me a lifelong passion for understanding the world around me. From a young age, I was driven by curiosity, a quality that led me to pursue a career in Physics. Throughout my journey, not only did I satisfy my thirst for knowledge, but I also discovered an endless playground for exploration. There's something exhilarating about tackling a new challenge, carefully planning a project, and diving into the complexities where models, methods, and tools are tested. However, what truly excites me is that, at the end of the process, the team grows in understanding, and we’re able to deliver real, tangible value to clients or colleagues.

I am fortunate to have had the guidance of outstanding mentors—many of them professors—who shared their time and knowledge with me, and who instilled in me values that have shaped my professional life. These include dedication, commitment, respect for schedules, collaboration, and perseverance. Perhaps the most valuable lesson I took from my academic years, though, was the importance of teamwork. Science, like any meaningful endeavor, is a collective pursuit, and the best work is often the result of collaborative effort. These principles have remained central to my career and continue to guide me as I take on new challenges.

The road of an Applied Scientist

Over the course of my Master's, I worked on two projects that highlight some of my key strengths and personal attributes. The first was my first real experience in a research team, where I had the opportunity to study the behavior of autonomous systems—specifically, the flight patterns of bird flocks. While this may seem abstract, it’s a classic example of how different systems can exhibit similar behaviors despite their apparent differences. Thanks to concepts from Statistical Mechanics, we discovered that such systems share common properties, which can help us extrapolate results from controlled environments to more complex, real-world scenarios. In our case, we used a Vicsek Simulation to explore how different dynamic models affect the flight patterns of bird flocks. The technical results were fascinating, but the real value for me came from applying my knowledge of Statistical Mechanics and analysis to a tangible problem, while also improving my programming skills in simulation.

The second project was a BusLine-Layout Optimization for my city’s public transportation system. I tend to be proactive in identifying new challenges, and at that time, I wanted to apply my skills in programming and analysis to a real-world issue. I began working on a model to optimize the layout of the bus lines, and soon, some friends and colleagues joined me. Our work progressed to the point where we were able to present our ideas to the transit council. Although they appreciated the project, they ultimately lacked the resources to implement it—something I now understand is a common hurdle in such initiatives.

My passion for using scientific methods to improve the world around me has only grown. Outside of my academic work, I’ve taken on freelance Data Science projects, seeking out opportunities that allow me to challenge myself while delivering meaningful results. These projects not only expanded my skill set but also taught me important lessons in customer service, project planning, and negotiation. The positive feedback and perfect rating I received on Freelancer attest to the success of these efforts.

From academic to applied research

In my final year of the Master’s program, I had the incredible opportunity to work with the High Energy Physics team at ATLAS - CERN from La Plata. There, we developed a deep neural network (DNN) model to enhance the photon identification process using image data; details here. The project was a resounding success—not only technically, but also in terms of the collaboration itself. It marked the beginning of a series of ongoing projects that would extend into my PhD work.
Working within the ATLAS collaboration was also an invaluable learning experience. It’s one of the world’s most prestigious scientific communities, and the collaborative dynamics are similar to those found in large organizations across any industry. The scale of the ATLAS project and its global scope brought me face-to-face with the complexities of team coordination, resource management, and maintaining scientific rigor in a large, diverse environment. These lessons are incredibly transferable and continue to influence my professional approach today.

I joined Wildlife Studios as a core member of a newly-formed team dedicated to supporting the Digital Marketing division. The main objective of the role was to maximize the ROI of marketing campaigns. To achieve this, I led several key initiatives, including:

  • Conducting A/B testing and control experiments to validate and predict campaign performance, in this project.
  • Developing tools to track and monitor critical KPIs that measured the effectiveness of marketing campaigns.
  • Building and deploying state-of-the-art machine learning models to identify high-revenue opportunities. More on that here.
In this capacity, I took on a leadership role, guiding the team both technically and strategically. This required regular collaboration with VP-level stakeholders and cross-functional leaders to ensure alignment on goals and execution. While my time at Wildlife Studios was highly rewarding, it eventually paved the way for an even more exciting opportunity at TRACTIAN.

When TRACTIAN reached out to me, the company was still in its early startup phase. Initially, my role was to develop machine learning models to detect anomalous behavior in time-series data—critical to the company's core product. TRACTIAN provides preventive alerts for industrial machines, and the models I built were responsible for early, accurate identification of potential failures, helping clients avoid costly downtime.
This project was especially exciting because it bridged applied physics and statistics. However, as I gained a deeper understanding of the product, I identified a number of blockers that were hindering the growth of the data department. These included a lack of team structure, inadequate infrastructure, and insufficient resources.
The data teams lacked clear roles and structure. Specifically, there were no distinct divisions between Data Engineering, Machine Learning Operations, Data Research, and Data Products teams. This led to everyone contributing to multiple projects, making it difficult to maintain a clear, unified vision. To address this, I introduced a more structured team framework and took on the role of Data Products Team Lead, focusing on performance optimization.
On the infrastructure side, we lacked a proper Data Lake to manage our multiple databases, a system to evaluate product performance in depth, and cleaned, labeled datasets for the Data Research team. I delegated the Data Lake issue to the Data Engineering team, as it fell within their scope. Meanwhile, as Performance Lead, I took responsibility for building frameworks and tools to assess model performance at various stages:

  • Evaluating individual model performance within the overall model suite.
  • Assessing performance at scale, both at the company level and in customer environments.
  • Analyzing customer feedback to refine our models and overall product quality.
Simultaneously, leveraging my background as an experimental physicist, I guided the Data Research team in addressing the dataset issue. This challenge required a multi-pronged approach:
  • We established a Lab to facilitate research and controlled dataset creation.
  • We used organic cases from our platform to build real-world datasets.
  • I developed a synthetic sample simulation engine to augment and bridge gaps between the two types of datasets.
These efforts were crucial in overcoming the barriers that initially hindered our progress and set the foundation for scaling the data department.
