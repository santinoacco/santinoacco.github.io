A brief recap of my professional journey

A summary of myself, my interests and achivements.

Santiago Noacco

Where did it all began

Born and raised in Patagonia, Argentina, I grew up deeply connected to nature, which I believe instilled in me a lifelong passion for understanding the world around me. From a young age, I was driven by curiosity, a quality that led me to pursue a career in Physics. Throughout my journey, not only did I satisfy my thirst for knowledge, but I also discovered an endless playground for exploration. There's something exhilarating about tackling a new challenge, carefully planning a project, and diving into the complexities where models, methods, and tools are tested. However, what truly excites me is that, at the end of the process, the team grows in understanding, and we’re able to deliver real, tangible value to clients or colleagues.

I am fortunate to have had the guidance of outstanding mentors—many of them professors—who shared their time and knowledge with me, and who instilled in me values that have shaped my professional life. These include dedication, commitment, respect for schedules, collaboration, and perseverance. Perhaps the most valuable lesson I took from my academic years, though, was the importance of teamwork. Science, like any meaningful endeavor, is a collective pursuit, and the best work is often the result of collaborative effort. These principles have remained central to my career and continue to guide me as I take on new challenges.

The road of an Applied Scientist

Over the course of my Master's, I worked on two projects that highlight some of my key strengths and personal attributes. The first was my first real experience in a research team, where I had the opportunity to study the behavior of autonomous systems—specifically, the flight patterns of bird flocks. While this may seem abstract, it’s a classic example of how different systems can exhibit similar behaviors despite their apparent differences. Thanks to concepts from Statistical Mechanics, we discovered that such systems share common properties, which can help us extrapolate results from controlled environments to more complex, real-world scenarios. In our case, we used a Vicsek Simulation to explore how different dynamic models affect the flight patterns of bird flocks. The technical results were fascinating, but the real value for me came from applying my knowledge of Statistical Mechanics and analysis to a tangible problem, while also improving my programming skills in simulation.

The second project was a BusLine-Layout Optimization for my city’s public transportation system. I tend to be proactive in identifying new challenges, and at that time, I wanted to apply my skills in programming and analysis to a real-world issue. I began working on a model to optimize the layout of the bus lines, and soon, some friends and colleagues joined me. Our work progressed to the point where we were able to present our ideas to the transit council. Although they appreciated the project, they ultimately lacked the resources to implement it—something I now understand is a common hurdle in such initiatives.

My passion for using scientific methods to improve the world around me has only grown. Outside of my academic work, I’ve taken on freelance Data Science projects, seeking out opportunities that allow me to challenge myself while delivering meaningful results. These projects not only expanded my skill set but also taught me important lessons in customer service, project planning, and negotiation. The positive feedback and perfect rating I received on Freelancer attest to the success of these efforts.

Academic and research experiences

In my final year of the Master’s program, I had the incredible opportunity to work with the High Energy Physics team at ATLAS - CERN from La Plata. There, we developed a deep neural network (DNN) model to enhance the photon identification process using image data. The project was a resounding success—not only technically, but also in terms of the collaboration itself. It marked the beginning of a series of ongoing projects that would extend into my PhD work. Working within the ATLAS collaboration was also an invaluable learning experience. It’s one of the world’s most prestigious scientific communities, and the collaborative dynamics are similar to those found in large organizations across any industry. The scale of the ATLAS project and its global scope brought me face-to-face with the complexities of team coordination, resource management, and maintaining scientific rigor in a large, diverse environment. These lessons are incredibly transferable and continue to influence my professional approach today.

I joint Wildlife Studios to become a member of a new small team that provided support for the Digital Marketing squads. The general premise of the role was to maximize the ROI of marketing campaigns. Achieving such goal would imply carrying out control experiments (A/B tests) to validate or project the performance of campaigns or their adjustments; developing tools to monitor the evolution of critical KPIs that described the performance of such campaings; and developing state-of-the-art ML models to find high revenue opportunities. In this role I was actively leading the team both from a technical and product point of view, which implied having close relationship with VP-level stakeholders and cross team leaders. The role was very rewarding, but not as much as the opportunity that would come after with TRACTIAN.
